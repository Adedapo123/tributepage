Dr. Norman Borlaug

The man who saved a billion lives.

Here's a time line of Dr. Borlaug's life


  • 1914 - Born in cresco, lowa

  • 1933 - leaves his family's farm to attend the University of Minnesota, thanks to a Depression era program known as the National Youth Administration

  • 1935 - Has to stop school to save up money. Works in the Civillian Conservation Corps, helping starving Americans. I saw how food change them, he said. All of this left scar on me.

  • 1937 - Finishes university and takes up a job in the US Forestry Service

  • 1938 - Marries wife of 69 years Magret Gibson. Get laid of due to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman, he return to schook to study under Stakman, who teaches him about breeding pest-resistent plants.

  • 1941 - Tries to enroll in the military after Pearl Habor attack, but is rejected. Instead, the military asked his lab to work on waterproof glue, DDT to control malaria, disinfectants, and ither applied science.

  • 1942 - Receives a Ph.D. in Genetics and Plant Pathology

  • 1944 - Reject a 100% salary increase from Dupont, leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years, his team breeds 6,000 different strains of disease resistent wheat - including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.

  • 1945 - Discovers a way to grown wheat twice each season, doubling wheat yields

  • 1953 - crosses a short, sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yeidling American breed, creating a strain that responds well to fertilizer. it goes on to provide 95% of Mexico's wheat

  • 1962 - Visits Delhi and brings his high-yieldling strains of wheat to the indian subcontinent in time to help mitigate mass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population

  • 1970 - recieves the Nobel Peace Prize

  • 1983 - helps seven African countries dramatically increase their maize and sorghum yields

  • 1984 - becomes a distinguished professor at Texas A&M University

  • 2005 - states we will have to double the world food supply by 2050. Argues that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet the demand, as we run out of arable land. says that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because we've been genetically modifying plants and animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people were selecting the best breeds.

  • 2009 - dies at the age of 95.
